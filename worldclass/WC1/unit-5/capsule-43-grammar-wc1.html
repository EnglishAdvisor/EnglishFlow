The rule — Adverbial Clauses
Adverbial clauses explain when, why, or in what way something happened. They begin with a connecting word. When the clause starts the sentence, use a comma. Book reference: SB page 51 + Grammar Summary p.159.
TypeConnectorsExample
Timeafter, since, whenever, as soon as, until, while, before, whenAs soon as the alarm rang, everyone left the building.
Reasonbecause, sinceShe stayed calm because she had a plan.
Contrastalthough, (even) thoughEven though it was hard, they never gave up.
Purposeso (that)They train every month so that nobody panics in a real emergency.
💡 before, after, during, since, until can also be followed by a noun phrase: During the storm, we stayed inside.
Activity A — Combine the sentences
Marcos hurt his knee playing football. Combine each pair into ONE sentence using the best connecting word in (italics):
1. I played the final match. I knew my knee was weak. (even though, until, so that)
2. I fell. I was running to the goal. (while, whenever, since)
3. I knew it was serious. I tried to stand up. (as soon as, although, so that)
4. My friend drove me to the hospital. I could see a doctor. (although, so that, while)
5. I can't play football. My knee is fully healed. (so that, until, whenever)
6. I'm going to play again. I feel better. (as soon as, even though, because)

Grammar at a glance
⏰ Time
as soon as = immediately when
while = during that time
until = up to that moment
whenever = every time that
🤔 Reason
because / since + reason
⚖️ Contrast
although / even though = "but" idea
🎯 Purpose
so (that) + goal
✏️ Comma rule
Clause first → comma: While I was running, I fell.
Clause second → no comma: I fell while I was running.
