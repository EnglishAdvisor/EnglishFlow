The upstream process — as a new hire would learn it
Before anyone drills anything, what happens first?
A team of geologists runs seismic surveys over the exploration area, reading sound waves bounced off the rock layers below the seabed — hunting for formations that might be trapping hydrocarbons.
Does a good seismic reading guarantee oil?
Not even close. It only narrows down where to try. The real test is drilling a well — and plenty of wells come back empty.
Why call it a "hydrocarbon" instead of just "oil"?
Because the molecule is built from just two elements — hydrogen and carbon. Crude oil, natural gas, even the gas that flares off a platform: all hydrocarbons, just different mixes of the same two atoms.
A well hits oil — does the platform start pumping the next day?
No. Engineers spend months, sometimes years, answering two questions: how much is actually down there, and can it be extracted safely. Only after that comes the next stage: development.
What gets built during development?
The heavy infrastructure — platforms, subsea equipment, and the pipeline network that will eventually carry everything to shore.
And after all that — is the field finally live?
Yes — that's production: crude oil and gas flowing continuously from the reservoir, up the well, and out through the pipeline.
