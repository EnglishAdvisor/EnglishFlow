Natural gas is a fossil fuel formed underground over millions of years from organic material. It is found in reservoirs below the Earth's surface, often in the same areas as oil and coal deposits.

The main component of natural gas is methane (CH₄). It also contains smaller amounts of ethane, propane, and butane, which are separated during processing and sold as valuable by-products.

Gas found alongside oil is called associated gas. Gas extracted from fields with little or no oil is called non-associated gas. After extraction, both types go to a processing plant to remove water and impurities.

Once cleaned, natural gas is transported through pipelines underground and across borders. When pipelines are not possible — for example, across oceans — the gas is cooled to -160°C and becomes LNG (Liquefied Natural Gas). Specially designed tanker ships carry LNG to markets worldwide.

Natural gas is one of the most versatile energy sources. It heats homes, generates electricity in power stations, and fuels industrial processes. It is also a key feedstock for the petrochemical industry, used to produce fertilizers, plastics, and chemicals.

Compared with coal and oil, burning natural gas produces fewer greenhouse gas emissions. This makes it an important part of the global energy transition. Today, natural gas supplies around 25% of the world's total energy.

Global demand for natural gas is growing rapidly, especially for LNG. Countries are investing in new pipelines, regasification terminals, and compression facilities to secure their energy supply and reduce dependence on coal.
