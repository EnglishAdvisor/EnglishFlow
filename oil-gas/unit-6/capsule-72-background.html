Background — Unit 6 Drilling

Wells are drilled using a drill bit — toothed wheels which turn and crush the rock. The bit is attached to a drill string, which also turns and is lowered from the surface. The drill string is made up of pipes, each around ten metres long and screwed together. There may be around a hundred pipes in a drill string.

The drill string is connected to a kelly, which is a square or hexagonal pipe that slides through the rotary table. The rotary table rotates the kelly and is turned by a powerful drive motor. The drilling derrick is a tall structure with a hoist — the crown block is fixed to the top, and the travelling block supports the kelly. This hoist lowers and raises the drill pipe in the well.

As the well is drilled deeper, more sections of pipe are added. Some modern rigs use a top drive to turn the pipe instead of a kelly and rotary table, because it can be safer and more efficient.

Drilling mud is pumped through the kelly and drill string to the drill bit to lubricate and cool the cutting area. It returns through the outside of the drill pipe, carrying the drill cuttings up to the surface and providing pressure between the drill pipe and the sides of the well. The mud is a mixture of clay and special additives.

The drilling crew includes roustabouts (the most junior workers who clean and move equipment), roughnecks (more skilled, they connect and disconnect heavy drill pipes), the derrickman (works high up on the derrick, looks after the mud equipment), the driller (supervises and controls the drilling equipment), and the toolpusher or rig manager (the most senior person, responsible for safety and paperwork).

Key parts — drilling
drill bit — toothed wheels that crush rock
drill string — long string of connected pipes
kelly — square pipe connecting rotary table to drill string
rotary table — rotates the kelly and drill string
derrick — tall steel structure supporting equipment
crown block — pulleys at top of derrick
mud pump — pumps drilling mud down the drill string
mud tank — holds the drilling mud
mud screen — separates cuttings from mud
cuttings — rock pieces from drilling
top drive — modern alternative to kelly/rotary table
Mud process
1 Pump pushes mud from tank
2 Mud flows down drill string
3 Cools & lubricates the bit
4 Mud + cuttings flow up
5 Screen separates cuttings
6 Clean mud returns to tank
