Oil companies employ geologists to find areas where oil and gas can be recovered. Geologists study how the earth was millions of years ago. They look for reservoirs — underground rocks that contain oil and gas. Scientists use special measuring devices to survey these areas, and the results help companies decide where to drill.

One important method is seismic surveying. Vibrator trucks create vibrations on the surface, and these vibrations send sound waves down through the rock layers below. Each rock layer reflects some of the waves back up to the surface.

Special receivers called geophones pick up the reflected waves and convert them into electrical signals. A machine in a recording truck records all the signals. Computers then process the signals and create detailed 3D maps of the rocks below the surface. Companies study these maps and look for possible oil traps.

At sea, the process is similar. Ships tow hydrophones fitted to cables or streamers. An underwater gun makes the seismic waves. The hydrophones pick up the reflected signals, and computers convert them into 3D maps.

Large survey teams often work in desert environments. They use a GPS — Global Positioning System — to navigate over wide areas and to position the geophones correctly. Good survey data saves a lot of money — because drilling in the wrong place is very expensive.
